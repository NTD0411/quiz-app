TRAVEL FROM VIETNAM TO CAMBODIA 
Vietnam and Cambodia are neighbors in Southeast Asia on the Indochina Peninsula. The two countries share a long border with a variety of crossings. Travel between the two countries is remarkably easy, with a variety of ways to travel, including flights, buses, cars and boats. 
Travel Documents
Visas are required to travel from Vietnam into Cambodia; however, they are available on arrival at airports and border crossings. In order to qualify for a visa on arrival, you must have two passport-sized photographs with you, although if you don't, you may simply be fined a small amount and granted a visa anyway. 
Flights
Cambodia has two major airports, one in the capital, Phnom Penh (airport code PNH), and one in Siem Reap (REP), which is near the Angkor Wat temple complex. Most flights from Vietnam to Cambodia originate in Ho Chi Minh City and take between one to two hours. Arriving by air in Cambodia is relatively simple and the customs process is mostly hassle-free.
Land Crossings
Numerous land crossings are available between Vietnam and Cambodia. Many people travel from Ho Chi Minh City to Phnom Penh by bus, a trip that takes approximately six hours, including a border stop. This crossing is called Moc Bai on the Vietnamese side and Bavet on the Cambodian side. Additionally, at least seven other land border crossings are open between the two nations; private vehicles are allowed to cross at all points. Visas on arrival are available at most land border crossings.
Water Crossings
The Mekong River runs north to south through Cambodia before ending in Vietnam. River boat border crossings are available with brief stops on land to check passports and visas. Visas on arrival are available at the Cambodian border crossing. Fast boat service is available from Chau Doc in Vietnam to Phnom Penh. More leisurely cruises are also available, and service is available all the way to Siem Reap in the north central part of Cambodia.

PASSPORT EXAMINATION
Millions of passports are examined every day to confirm the identity and nationality of the holders. A quick conversation often helps to resolve or identify problems quickly. 
The first qualitative assessment done by the examiner concerns the cover of the passport. Traditionally the cover has the name of the issuing state and a well-defined image such as the national coat-of-arms printed in gold or silver. The text and images in a counterfeit document are usually far less precise. 
The next step is to briefly examine the construction of the document. Begin with a check of pages, ensuring that they are all there, the same size and that all the edges are even.
